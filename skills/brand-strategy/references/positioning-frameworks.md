# Positioning Frameworks Reference

## The April Dunford Method (Adapted for Personal Brands)

Original method is for products. Adapted here for personal brand positioning.

### Step 1: List Your Competitive Alternatives
What would your audience do if you didn't exist?
- Follow competitor A
- Read generic blog posts
- Take an online course
- Figure it out themselves
- Hire a consultant

### Step 2: Isolate Your Unique Attributes
What do you offer that the alternatives don't?
- Specific experience (e.g., "built 3 startups from 0 to $1M")
- Unique combination of skills (e.g., "engineer who can write")
- Contrarian perspective (e.g., "growth without paid ads")
- Access or network (e.g., "interviews with founders weekly")
- Teaching style (e.g., "complex topics in 2-minute threads")

### Step 3: Map Attributes to Audience Value
For each unique attribute, answer: "So what? Why does the audience care?"
- Attribute: "Built 3 startups" → Value: "I've made the mistakes so you don't have to"
- Attribute: "Engineer who writes" → Value: "I explain technical topics without jargon"

### Step 4: Define Best-Fit Audience
Who cares MOST about these specific values?
Narrow ruthlessly. "Marketing professionals" is too broad.
"Solo B2B founders doing their own marketing for the first time" is focused.

### Step 5: Choose Your Category
What mental bucket does your audience put you in?
Options for personal brands:
- Educator ("I teach X")
- Practitioner ("I do X and share what I learn")
- Curator ("I find and filter the best X")
- Analyst ("I break down X with data")
- Storyteller ("I share stories about X")

### Step 6: Layer a Relevant Trend (Optional)
If a trend from the Research Brief aligns with your positioning, ride it.
But never build positioning ON a trend -- trends fade.

## The Positioning Spectrum

Map where the user's brand should sit on these spectrums:

```
Tactical ←————————→ Strategic
(how-to, step-by-step)    (frameworks, mental models)

Beginner ←————————→ Advanced
(101-level, accessible)    (deep, assumes knowledge)

Data-Driven ←————————→ Story-Driven
(charts, metrics, proof)   (narratives, anecdotes, emotion)

Broad ←————————→ Niche
(many topics, wide appeal)  (one topic, deep expertise)

Frequent ←————————→ Infrequent
(daily presence, volume)    (weekly/monthly, quality)
```

The user should NOT be in the middle on any spectrum. The middle is forgettable.
Pick a side. Commit. The further from center, the more memorable.

## Positioning Statement Template

```
FOR [specific audience with a specific problem]
I am the [category] who [unique approach]
UNLIKE [competitive alternative]
I [key differentiator]
BECAUSE [credibility proof]
```

## Positioning Validation Checklist

A positioning statement is strong if:
- [ ] A stranger can repeat it after hearing it once
- [ ] It would be FALSE for at least 80% of competitors
- [ ] It names a specific audience (not "everyone" or "professionals")
- [ ] It implies a clear content direction (you know what topics to cover)
- [ ] The user is genuinely excited about it (they'll need to live it daily)

## Anti-Patterns in Personal Brand Positioning

- **The Generalist:** "I help people succeed" — too vague, no one searches for this
- **The Copycat:** Same positioning as the market leader — you'll always be #2
- **The Resume:** "10 years in marketing" — credentials aren't positioning
- **The Platform:** "I post on LinkedIn" — channel is not strategy
- **The Pivot:** Changing positioning every month — audience can't follow
