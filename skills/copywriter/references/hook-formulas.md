# Hook Formulas

A bank of proven hook formulas for executive thought leadership content.
Select hooks based on content type and platform. Never use the same hook
formula for consecutive posts.

---

## Core Hook Formulas (from Strategy)

1. **Contrarian:** "Most people think [common belief]. They're wrong."
2. **Number:** "[X] things I learned after [experience/timeframe]."
3. **Story:** "In 2019, I was sitting in a boardroom in [city] when..."
4. **Question:** "Why do [X% of companies] still [outdated practice]?"
5. **Result:** "We [action] and [specific measurable result]."
6. **Curation:** "The best [thing] I [read/saw/heard] this week:"
7. **Framework:** "I use a [number]-step framework for [outcome]. Here it is:"
8. **Hot Take:** "[Strong opinion about industry trend]. Let me explain."
9. **Before/After:** "[Time period] ago, I [old state]. Today, [new state]. Here's what changed:"
10. **Mistake:** "The biggest mistake I see [audience] making with [topic]:"

## Executive Audience Hooks (5 additional)

11. **The Insider:** "Most executives won't tell you this, but [insider knowledge]."
12. **The Data Drop:** "New data just dropped: [stat]. Here's what it means:"
13. **The Counter-Narrative:** "The conventional wisdom on [topic] is wrong. Here's the data:"
14. **The Credential:** "After leading [X teams/projects] through [Y challenge], here's the #1 lesson:"
15. **The Prediction:** "By 2027, [specific prediction]. Here's why I'm betting on it:"

## Hooks by Content Type

### Best Hooks for TEACH Content (educational, frameworks)

| Hook Formula | Why It Works |
|---|---|
| Framework | Promises structured, actionable knowledge |
| Number | Sets expectations for scope and format |
| Mistake | Creates urgency through fear of getting it wrong |
| The Credential | Establishes authority before teaching |
| The Counter-Narrative | Challenges assumptions, makes reader curious |

Example: "I use a 3-part framework to evaluate AI creative tools. Most companies skip step 2 -- and it costs them."

### Best Hooks for SHOW Content (case studies, behind-the-scenes)

| Hook Formula | Why It Works |
|---|---|
| Story | Opens a narrative loop the reader must close |
| Result | Leads with proof, then explains |
| Before/After | Shows transformation with implicit "how" |
| The Insider | Promises access to non-public information |
| The Data Drop | Leads with evidence, builds credibility |

Example: "In 2023, my team was producing 200 assets a month. By Q4, we were doing 2,000 -- with the same headcount. Here's what actually happened."

### Best Hooks for ASK Content (engagement, polls)

| Hook Formula | Why It Works |
|---|---|
| Question | Directly invites response |
| Hot Take | Provokes agreement or disagreement |
| Contrarian | Forces the reader to take a position |
| The Prediction | Invites debate about the future |

Example: "Hot take: 90% of 'AI strategies' at creative agencies are just efficiency plays dressed up as transformation. Agree or disagree?"

### Best Hooks for SHARE Content (curation, commentary)

| Hook Formula | Why It Works |
|---|---|
| Curation | Signals you've filtered the noise |
| The Data Drop | Gives the share a reason to exist |
| The Counter-Narrative | Adds tension to the shared content |

Example: "The best article I read this week is about something nobody in our industry is talking about yet:"

## Hook Testing Criteria

Before committing to a hook, it must pass all four tests:

1. **CMO Test:** Would a Chief Marketing Officer stop scrolling for this?
2. **Information Gap:** Does it create a gap between what the reader knows and what they want to know?
3. **Specificity Test:** Is it specific (numbers, names, timeframes) rather than generic?
4. **Value Promise:** Does it promise value in the first 5 words?

If a hook fails any test, revise or choose a different formula.

## Anti-Patterns: Hooks That Fail for Executive Audiences

**Never use these approaches:**

- Clickbait: "You won't BELIEVE what happened next..."
- ALL CAPS for emphasis: "THIS is the future of creative."
- Excessive emojis as hooks: "// The secret to AI governance"
- Vague promises: "I learned something important this week."
- Self-congratulation: "Thrilled to announce..." (save for genuine milestones)
- Humble brag: "I never expected this post to go viral, but..."
- Generic motivation: "Every expert was once a beginner."
- Asking without giving: "What do you think about AI?" (no context, no stake)

**Why they fail:** Executive audiences have high noise filters. They scan for
signal -- specific data, credible experience, actionable insight. Anything that
smells like engagement farming gets scrolled past immediately.
