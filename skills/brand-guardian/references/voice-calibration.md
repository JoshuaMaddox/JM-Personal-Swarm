# Voice Calibration Reference

Use this document as the tuning fork for voice consistency reviews. Every example is a reference point for what the brand sounds like — and what it does not.

---

## On-Brand / Off-Brand Example Pairs

### Pair 1: Executive Authority vs. Generic Commentary

- **On-brand:** "When I restructured the creative team in Singapore, we had 48 hours to decide which roles would integrate AI tooling and which wouldn't. That decision framework is what I now teach every C-suite leader I advise."
- **Off-brand:** "AI is changing creative teams everywhere. Leaders need to think carefully about how to integrate new tools into their workflows."
- **Why:** The on-brand version is first person, specific (Singapore, 48 hours, decision framework), and positions the author as someone who has done this. The off-brand version could be written by anyone with a LinkedIn account. **Voice rule: specificity over abstraction.**

### Pair 2: Direct Recommendation vs. Hedging

- **On-brand:** "Stop running AI pilots. Pilots are where AI strategy goes to die. Pick one creative workflow, commit to full integration for 90 days, and measure. I've seen this work in agencies on 4 continents."
- **Off-brand:** "There are many approaches to AI adoption. Some organizations prefer pilots, while others go for full integration. It really depends on your context and resources."
- **Why:** The on-brand version takes a clear stance, backs it with experience, and tells you what to do. The off-brand version hedges, offers no opinion, and wastes the reader's time. **Voice rule: decide, don't hedge.**

### Pair 3: Warm Authority vs. Cold Corporate

- **On-brand:** "I got this wrong in Tokyo. We rolled out AI tools without asking the creative directors what they actually needed. The backlash taught me something I now consider non-negotiable: governance starts with the people who make the work, not the people who manage the budget."
- **Off-brand:** "It is essential that organizations implement AI governance frameworks that incorporate stakeholder input from creative professionals to ensure alignment with operational objectives."
- **Why:** The on-brand version admits a mistake (warmth), names a place (specificity), and delivers a principle through story. The off-brand version is corporate jargon that says the same thing with zero personality. **Voice rule: warm authority, not corporate distance.**

### Pair 4: Data-Backed Claim vs. Unsourced Assertion

- **On-brand:** "McKinsey's 2025 State of AI report found that 67% of creative organizations have no formal AI governance policy. I've seen this firsthand — of the 12 agencies I've consulted for this year, exactly 2 had written guidelines."
- **Off-brand:** "Most creative organizations don't have AI governance policies. This is a huge problem that needs to be addressed immediately."
- **Why:** The on-brand version cites a source, adds personal data, and builds credibility through layered evidence. The off-brand version makes a claim with no backing and uses alarm language instead of proof. **Voice rule: cite sources, layer evidence.**

### Pair 5: Pillar-Specific Angle vs. Broad AI Content

- **On-brand:** "The gap between how Lagos and London agencies are adopting AI isn't about technology access — it's about whose creative standards get encoded into the default. That's the global creative AI divide no one is talking about."
- **Off-brand:** "AI is being adopted differently around the world. Some regions are ahead, some are behind. It's interesting to watch how this plays out."
- **Why:** The on-brand version names specific cities, identifies a specific tension (whose standards get encoded), and connects to Pillar 2 with a sharp angle. The off-brand version is vague observation with no insight. **Voice rule: sharp pillar angles, not broad topics.**

## Platform-Specific Voice Adjustments

The voice stays the same across platforms. What changes is the register.

### LinkedIn — Polished and Structured
- Slightly longer sentences are acceptable
- Can include more context-setting before the main point
- Professional but never stiff — still first person, still specific
- Hooks should be bold statements or surprising data points
- Structure with line breaks for readability

### X (Twitter) — Conversational and Punchy
- Shorter sentences. Fragments are fine.
- Get to the point faster — the hook IS the tweet
- More willingness to be provocative or contrarian
- Threads can build an argument, but each tweet must stand alone
- No hashtag stuffing. One relevant hashtag maximum.

**Same voice, different register.** A LinkedIn post might open with a two-sentence story. The same idea on X opens with the punchline.

## Signature Phrase Pairing Guide

Use signature phrases naturally. Never force them. Here's which phrases pair best with which pillars:

| Phrase | Best Pillar | Usage Context |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| "AI limbo" | Pillar 1 (AI Creative Governance) | Organizations stuck between old workflows and AI integration, unable to commit to either |
| "Quality as strategy" | Pillar 3 (Creative Authenticity) | When arguing that maintaining creative standards is a competitive advantage, not a cost |
| "4 continents" | Pillar 2 (Global Creative AI Divide) | When drawing on cross-cultural experience to illustrate regional differences |
| "Governance starts with the makers" | Pillar 1 (AI Creative Governance) | When advocating for bottom-up AI policy in creative organizations |
| "The divide no one is talking about" | Pillar 2 (Global Creative AI Divide) | When surfacing an inequality in AI access or adoption between regions |

## Calibration Test Sentences

Rate each sentence 1-5 for brand alignment. Use these as your tuning fork when reviewing content.

### Sentence 1
> "AI will transform every creative industry within five years."

**Rating: 1/5.** Generic prediction with no specificity, no personal experience, no named examples. Reads like a conference keynote filler slide. Fails positioning, voice, and the red flag scan (hype-adjacent).

### Sentence 2
> "I've watched three agencies in Nairobi build AI-first creative workflows that outperform teams in New York using ten times the budget."

**Rating: 5/5.** First person, specific cities, specific claim (outperform, 10x budget), maps to Pillar 2 (Global Creative AI Divide), executive perspective. This is the voice.

### Sentence 3
> "There are definitely pros and cons to implementing AI governance in creative teams. It really depends on the organization's size and culture."

**Rating: 1/5.** Pure hedging. "It depends" without a recommendation. No opinion, no authority, no specificity. The brand takes stances — this takes none.

### Sentence 4
> "When we implemented creative AI governance at my last organization, approval cycles dropped 40% — but the real win was that the creative directors actually trusted the output."

**Rating: 4/5.** First person, specific result (40%), insight beyond the data (trust was the real win), Pillar 1 mapping. Loses one point for "my last organization" — should name the company or at least the city/region for full specificity.

### Sentence 5
> "Hot take: most AI-for-creatives tools are solving problems creative directors never asked about. I've surveyed 30+ creative leaders this quarter. Their #1 request isn't generation — it's governance."

**Rating: 5/5.** Direct, opinionated, backed by data (30+ leaders surveyed), subverts expectations (governance over generation), maps to Pillar 1, conversational register appropriate for X. This is what on-brand looks like on Twitter.
